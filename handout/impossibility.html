The theorem

No algorithm can satisfy both predictive parity and classification parity so long as the base rates of the two groups are different.

Proof

Even though the theorem isnâ€™t difficult to proveâ€”it is just a rewording of Bayesâ€™ theoremâ€”its significance is profound. It is worth following the proof carefully. The proof consist of two parts.

The first part establishes the following claim:

\[ \frac{PPV}{1-PPV} =\frac{p}{1-p} \times \frac{1- FNR}{FPR} \] where \(p\) stands for the base rate or prevalence of the outcome of interest in a given population.

Letâ€™s see why this claim holds. By definition, we know that

\[PPV = \frac{TP}{TP+FP} \tag{*}\] Notice that

\[TP = p \times TPR \text{ and } FP = (1-p)\times FPR \] where \(TPR\) is True Positive Rate (defined as the fraction of people who are correctly labeled â€˜positiveâ€™ out of all positive people) and \(FPR\) is False Positive Rate (defined as the fraction of people who are incorrectly labeled â€˜positiveâ€™ out of all negative people).

After replacing \(TP\) by \(p \times TPR\) and replacing \(FP\) by \((1-p)\times FPR\) in equation (*) above, we get:

\[PPV = \frac{p \times TPR}{p \times TPR+(1-p)\times FPR}.\]

We analogously define the complement of \(PPV\), as follows:

\[1-PPV = \frac{FP}{TP+FP}\]

Thus, by performing the appropriate replacements, we get:

\[1-PPV =\frac{(1-p)\times FPR}{p \times TPR+(1-p)\times FPR}.\]

Finally, we take the ratio of \(PPV\) and its complement, as follows: The rightmost equality uses the fact that \(TPR=1-FNR\)

\[ \frac{PPV}{1-PPV} = \frac{ \frac{p \times TPR}{p \times TPR+(1-p)\times FPR}}{\frac{(1-p)\times FPR}{p \times TPR+(1-p)\times FPR}}=\frac{p}{1-p} \times \frac{TPR}{FPR} = \frac{p}{1-p} \times \frac{1- FNR}{FPR} \]

We are now ready to embark on the second part of the proof, the one that establishes the impossibility. The equation we just established holds for any group (indices 1 and 2):

\[ \frac{PPV_1}{1-PPV_1}= \frac{p_1}{1-p_1} \times \frac{1-FNR_1}{FPR_1}\] \[ \frac{PPV_2}{1-PPV_2}= \frac{p_2}{1-p_2} \times \frac{1-FNR_2}{FPR_2} \]

By inspecting the two equations, we can see that predictive parity (equality in \(PPV\)) and classification parity (here understood as equality in error rates, \(FNR\) and \(FPR\)) cannot be concurrently satisfied whenever the base rate or prevalence \(p\) differs across groups.

Suppose the base rates of the two groups are different, hence \(\frac{p_1}{1-p_1} \neq \frac{p_2}{1-p_2}\). If \(\frac{PPV_2}{1-PPV_2}=\frac{PPV_1}{1-PPV_1}\) (predictive parity is satisfied), then \(\frac{1-FNR_1}{FPR_1}\neq \frac{1-FNR_2}{FPR_2}\) (classification parity is violated). Conversely, if \(\frac{1-FNR_1}{FPR_1} = \frac{1-FNR_2}{FPR_2}\) (classification parity is satisfied), then \(\frac{PPV_2}{1-PPV_2} \neq \frac{PPV_1}{1-PPV_1}\) (predictive parity is violated). QED.

Kleinberg et alâ€™s impossibility theorem

This theorem2 Kleinberg, Mullainathan and Raghavan (2016), Inherent Trade-Offs in the Fair Determination of Risk Scores relies on slightly different fairness criteria:

Intuitvely, the risk score mean the same thing (=likelihood of the outcome, say recidivism) across groups.

  1. Predictive Calibration - The fraction of positive people out of those who are assigned the same risk score is the same across groups. In other words, the score reflects the same likelihood of the outcome across groups.

  2. Classification Balance - Positive individuals are assigned the same average risk score across groups (positive balance). Negative people are assigned the same average score across groups (negative balance).

Predictive calibration and classification balance are similar to predictive parity and classification parity. But they are criteria for the fairness of algorithmic scores rather than algorithmic decisions. These criteria of fairness can be applied independently of the choice of a decision threshold.

The theorem states that, given different base rates or prevalence across groups, no algorithm can satisfy both predictive calibration and classification balance unless the algorithm achieves perfect prediction. The proof is involved.

The starting point of the paper are two families of fairness criteria, those based on equality of predictive value (or predictive accuracy) and those based on equality in error rates. The paper makes three claims: conceptual, normative and legal.

  1. Conceptual claim

Non-comparative. Predictive value (or predictive accuracy) matters for how we should form our beliefs. For example, if a student passes a test and the test has high predictive accuracy, we should be confident that the student is actually well-prepared. If the test has low predictive accuracy, we should not be confident that the student is actually well-prepared.

Comparative. Like predictive accuracy, equal predictive value (EPV) matters for how we should form our beliefs. If a test has less predictive accuracy for men than for women (see example on page 832), we should be less confident that a man who passed the test is actually well-prepared than how confident we should be that a woman who also passed the same test is actually well-prepared.

Does this difference imply that men have been treated unfairly or that women have been treated unfairly? It seems not. Fair treatment pertains to decisions and actions, and instead, equal predictive value guides belief formation.

  1. Normative claim

Non-comparative. When we make a decisionâ€”such as convict someone or put someone in preventative detentionâ€”we could make a false positive decision or false negative decision. Before we make a decision, we should weigh the these types of mistakes against one another and strike the right balance between false positives and false negatives.

Comparative. Fairness requires that, when we make a decision, we strike the same balance between false positives and false negatives across groups. In other words, fairness requires we comply with error ratio parity (ERP). The violation of error ratio parity (ERP) is â€˜importantly suggestiveâ€™ (p.Â 836) of unfairness.

Both EPV and ERP are important. An algorithm should probably satisfy both. But only the latter matters for assessing the fairness of decisions and actions. The former matters for forming equally accurate beliefs.
